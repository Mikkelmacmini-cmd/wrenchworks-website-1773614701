Memory.md - Beginner Course (6th-grade level)

What you'll find here:

  • A simple explanation of what MEMORY.md is and why it matters
  • How to keep tacit knowledge (thinking habits, preferences, lessons learned)
  • Rules for when to add or merge facts
  • Examples and short exercises you can do
  • A template to create your own MEMORY.md entries
  • A one-page cheat-sheet for quick reference

Goal: By the end you'll understand how to capture and care for tacit knowledge so it helps you and your team remember important ways you work.

Lesson: What is MEMORY.md?

  1. Simple definition MEMORY.md is a place to write down "how we do things" — not facts about the world, but the habits, preferences, patterns, and lessons a person or team uses. It's like a cookbook for how you think and work.

  2. Why it matters

  • Stops us from repeating mistakes
  • Helps new people learn how we like to work
  • Keeps small but important knowledge from disappearing
  1. What goes in MEMORY.md
  • Patterns: "We always run tests before merging"
  • Preferences: "Use 2-space indentation" or "Morning standups at 9:15"
  • Lessons learned: "Deploying on Fridays caused outages; avoid it"
  • Decision rules: "If two team members disagree, the owner decides"
  1. What does NOT belong
  • Random facts about the world (use items.json or notes)
  • Sensitive secrets (passwords, tokens)

  1. Easy example (story) Imagine you and a friend build a treehouse. After some mistakes, you write down: "Measure twice, cut once" and "Always wear gloves when using the saw." That's MEMORY.md — lessons so you don't forget next time.

  2. How to read and use it

  • Read MEMORY.md when onboarding someone
  • Check it before making big changes
  • Update it when you learn something new about how you work
  1. Short checklist for updates
  • Is this about how we work or a world fact? If how we work → add it
  • Is it simply replacing an older preference? Mark the old one superseded
  • Is it time-sensitive? Note the date and reason

How to maintain tacit knowledge (step-by-step)

  1. Capture quickly
  • When you notice a pattern, preference, or lesson, jot it down right away in MEMORY.md (or a draft). Use simple sentences.
  1. Be concrete
  • Write the behavior: "We ask for two reviewers on PRs" instead of vague notes.
  1. Use timestamps and sources
  • Add a small note like: (2026-03-11) — learned after outage during deploy
  1. Keep it short
  • One idea per line. Long stories go in daily notes; MEMORY.md keeps the distilled rule.
  1. Review regularly
  • Weekly or monthly quick pass: anything outdated? Mark as superseded with a link to the new rule.
  1. Merge carefully
  • When two similar entries exist, merge into one clearer rule and mark the older with status: superseded and point to the merged id.
  1. Who edits?
  • Anyone can suggest edits, but major changes should note the author and date.
  1. Never delete
  • Instead mark status: superseded and add supersededBy to keep history.

  1. Example entry id: tacit-001 fact: "We release to production only on Tuesdays after passing the full test suite." category: procedure timestamp: 2026-03-11 source: post-mortem-2026-03-11 status: active

  2. Quick rules summary

  • Capture fast, write clear, review often, never delete — supersede.

When to add facts

Add a fact to MEMORY.md when:

  • It describes how you or your team works (processes, preferences, rules)
  • You or multiple people notice the same pattern repeatedly (3+ times)
  • It affects future decisions (deployment rules, meeting norms)
  • You learned a lesson worth remembering (post-mortem conclusion)

When to merge facts

Merge when:

  • Two entries say the same thing in different words
  • You have overlapping rules that cause confusion
  • A newer rule replaces and clarifies an older one

How to merge safely

  • Create the merged entry with a new id
  • Copy or summarize key details from both
  • Update older entries: set status: superseded and add supersededBy:
  • Add a short note explaining why you merged

Example merge

  • old: "Deploy on Tuesdays" and "No Friday deploys"
  • merged: "Deploy only on Tuesdays; never deploy on Fridays (superseded: deploy-2025-11)"

Examples (simple and useful)

  1. Preference
  • fact: "Use 2-space indentation for code." (category: preference)
  1. Pattern
  • fact: "We open a short RFC for design decisions that change the API." (category: process)
  1. Lesson learned
  • fact: "Hotfixing on production without tests caused data loss; require tests for hotfixes." (category: lesson)
  1. Decision rule
  • fact: "If a security issue is found, escalate to the on-call and pause feature releases." (category: rule)
  1. Small template example (single line)
  • id: pref-001 fact: "Morning standups at 9:15" category: preference timestamp: 2026-03-11 status: active
  1. Real short case study (story) Team had repeat outages because deploy happened late Friday. They wrote: "Never deploy on Fridays; schedule deploys for Tuesdays after tests pass." Problem solved.

Exercises (short, for practice)

  1. Spot the tacit knowledge Read these items and decide which belong in MEMORY.md and why:
  • "The server IP is 10.0.0.5" (no)
  • "We always tag releases with semantic versioning" (yes)
  • "Alice prefers async messages in the morning" (yes)

  1. Write a rule from a story Think of a time something went wrong. Write a one-line rule for MEMORY.md that would prevent it.

  2. Merge two entries Given:

  • "Run full tests before deploy"
  • "Deploy only after code review and tests" Create a single clear rule and mark which old entries are superseded.

  1. Create an entry using the template (see template.md) Fill in the fields and save it.

  2. Review practice Take three entries from older notes and decide: keep, edit, or supersede. Write a one-line reason for your choice.

MEMORY.md entry template (use this for new entries)

id: fact: "A single clear sentence describing the tacit knowledge" category: preference|process|lesson|rule timestamp: YYYY-MM-DD source: <where you learned it or which meeting/post-mortem> status: active|superseded supersededBy: relatedEntities: [optional list] notes:

Example filled: id: deploy-002 fact: "Deploy to production only on Tuesdays after full test suite passes." category: process timestamp: 2026-03-11 source: post-mortem-2026-03-11 status: active notes: "Changed after two outages in Oct/Nov."

One-page cheat-sheet

  • What to save: How we work, not world facts
  • How to write: One idea per entry; be concrete
  • When to add: When a pattern emerges (3+ times) or a decision affects future work
  • When to merge: Similar entries or clarifications; mark old as superseded
  • Never delete: mark status: superseded and keep history
  • Review cadence: Weekly quick pass; full rewrite monthly or when many changes
  • Quick template fields: id, fact, category, timestamp, source, status, supersededBy

Remember: MEMORY.md helps people learn how you think. Keep it simple and useful.
